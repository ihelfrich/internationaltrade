Section I

Ricardian Model

Technology differences, comparative advantage, and gains.

Core Assumptions

  • One factor of production: labor (L)
  • Perfect competition and constant returns
  • Labor mobile across sectors, immobile across countries
  • Technology summarized by unit labor requirements

Setup

\[a_{LC} Q_C + a_{LW} Q_W \le L\]
  • PPF is linear with slope \(-a_{LW}/a_{LC}\)
  • Opportunity cost of W in terms of C is \(a_{LW}/a_{LC}\)

PPF Geometry

C QW Specialization point

Autarky relative price equals the PPF slope.

Comparative Advantage

\[\frac{a_{LC}}{a_{LW}} < \frac{a^*_{LC}}{a^*_{LW}}\Rightarrow\text{Home has CA in C}\]
  • Trade price must lie between autarky prices
  • Specialization delivers gains even if one country is more productive in both goods

World Relative Supply and Demand

Relative Supply (RS)

Vertical under complete specialization.

Relative Demand (RD)

Downward sloping in \(P_W/P_C\).

Gains from Trade

  • Production moves to the specialization point
  • Consumption lies on a world-price budget line
  • Budget line is flatter than autarky PPF
  • Consumption set expands beyond the PPF

Real Wages

\[w = \frac{P_C}{a_{LC}} = \frac{P_W}{a_{LW}}\]
  • \(w/P_C\) fixed by technology
  • \(w/P_W\) rises for the export country
  • Single-factor economy implies no distributional conflict

Ricardian Limits

  • Complete specialization is strong; reality is more mixed
  • No role for multiple factors or distributional conflict
  • Trade costs and scale are outside the model
Section II

Specific-Factors Model

Short-run distribution and political economy.

Structure

  • Three factors: labor (L), capital (K), land (T)
  • Labor mobile across sectors; K and T are sector-specific
  • Diminishing MPL in each sector

Production Functions

\[Q_M = F(K, L_M), \quad Q_A = G(T, L_A)\]
\[P_M \cdot MPL_M = P_A \cdot MPL_A = w\]

Concave PPF

Diminishing returns to labor make the PPF bowed out.

Price Shock: \(P_M\) Rises

  • Labor shifts into manufacturing
  • \(MPL_M\) falls, \(MPL_A\) rises
  • Wage rises, but less than \(P_M\)

Distributional Effects

Capital (M)

Real return rises unambiguously.

Land (A)

Real return falls unambiguously.

Labor

Ambiguous: depends on consumption weights.

Relative Supply

  • Upward sloping in \(P_M/P_A\)
  • Trade equilibrium: RS intersects RD
  • Incomplete specialization is typical

Policy Lens

  • Tariff on agriculture raises \(P_A/P_M\)
  • Landowners gain; capital loses; labor ambiguous
  • Explains industry-based coalitions
Section III

Gravity Model

Trade volumes, frictions, and structural counterfactuals.

Basic Gravity Equation

\[X_{ij} = G \cdot \frac{Y_i^a Y_j^b}{D_{ij}^c}\]
  • Flows rise with economic size and fall with trade costs
  • Distance proxies for freight, time, and information frictions

Log-Linear Form

\[\ln X_{ij} = g + a\ln Y_i + b\ln Y_j - c\ln D_{ij} + Z_{ij}\gamma\]
  • \(Z_{ij}\): border, language, agreement, tariff variables
  • Estimated with exporter and importer fixed effects

Structural Gravity

\[X_{ij} = \frac{Y_i E_j}{Y_W} \left(\frac{\tau_{ij}}{P_i P_j}\right)^{1-\sigma}\]
  • \(P_i, P_j\) are multilateral resistance terms
  • Trade is relative: bilateral costs vs. all other options

Multilateral Resistance Intuition

If everyone else is far from j, i trades more with j.

Estimation Practice

  • Exporter and importer fixed effects
  • Bilateral controls for trade costs
  • PPML for zeros and heteroskedasticity

Section IV

Tariffs and Protection

Supply, demand, and real-world policy effects.

Supply, Demand, and World Price

  • Small country takes world price P* as given
  • Imports = QD(P*) − QS(P*)

Tariff Wedge

  • Domestic price rises by t
  • Imports fall; government collects tariff revenue

Effects of a Tariff

  • Consumer surplus decreases
  • Producer surplus increases
  • Government revenue increases
  • Deadweight loss from distorted production and consumption

Small Country Tariff

  • World price is unchanged (no terms-of-trade effect)
  • Welfare loss equals deadweight loss
  • DWL ≈ 0.5 · t · (ΔQD + ΔQS)

Effective Rate of Protection

\\[ERP = \\frac{t_f - a t_i}{1 - a}\\]
  • tf: tariff on final good; ti: tariff on inputs
  • a: share of imported inputs in final-good value
  • ERP can exceed nominal tariffs when inputs are lightly taxed

Measuring the Amount of Protection

  • Nominal tariff rate (ad valorem or specific)
  • Ad valorem equivalents for quotas and standards
  • Coverage ratios: share of trade lines affected
  • Price wedge: domestic price − world price

Short-Run vs Long-Run Costs

  • Short run: output and employment shift toward protected sectors
  • Higher input costs for downstream firms
  • Retaliation reduces export demand
  • Long run: lower productivity and slower diffusion of technology

U.S. Tariffs in 2018–2020

  • By Feb 2020, average U.S. tariffs on Chinese exports were about 19.3%
  • Coverage reached roughly 66.6% of U.S. imports from China (2017 base, about $335B)
  • China’s retaliatory tariffs averaged about 21% and covered ~58.3% of U.S. exports to China

Source: PIIE (Bown), “US-China Trade War Tariffs: An Up-to-Date Chart” (piie.com/research/piie-charts/us-china-trade-war-tariffs-date-chart).

  • Tariffs were largely passed through to U.S. import prices
