Specific-Factors Model — Full Lecture

Short-run distribution, factor specificity, and policy incentives.

Setup Equilibrium Price Shock Returns Trade Worked Example

1) Setup

Intuition

  • Two sectors: manufacturing (M), agriculture (A).
  • Labor is mobile across sectors.
  • Capital (K) specific to M, land (T) specific to A.

Math

\(Q_M = F(K, L_M), \quad Q_A = G(T, L_A)\)
\(L_M + L_A = L\)

2) Equilibrium conditions

Firms hire labor until the value of marginal product equals the wage:

\(P_M MPL_M = w, \quad P_A MPL_A = w\)

So labor allocation is pinned down by:

\(P_M MPL_M = P_A MPL_A\)

Because MPLs are diminishing, the PPF is concave.

3) Price shock mechanics

  1. Suppose \(P_M\) rises.
  2. Manufacturing becomes more profitable → labor shifts to M.
  3. \(MPL_M\) falls, \(MPL_A\) rises.
  4. Wage rises, but by less than \(P_M\).
Result: labor’s real wage is ambiguous — it can buy more A but less M.

4) Returns to specific factors

\(r_K = P_M MPK_M, \quad r_T = P_A MPT_A\)

If \(P_M\) rises, \(r_K\) increases more than proportionally (magnification), while \(r_T\) falls.

Specific factor in the expanding sector gains. Specific factor in the contracting sector loses.

5) Opening to trade

If the country has comparative advantage in M, world prices raise \(P_M/P_A\). The distributional effects mirror the price shock:

  • Capital owners gain.
  • Landowners lose.
  • Workers face ambiguous effects.
This model explains why trade politics are industry-based in the short run.

6) Worked example (directional)

Assume Cobb–Douglas technologies:

\(Q_M = K^{0.4}L_M^{0.6}, \quad Q_A = T^{0.4}L_A^{0.6}\)

A 10% rise in \(P_M\) induces labor reallocation into M:

  • \(w\) rises less than 10%
  • \(r_K\) rises more than 10%
  • \(r_T\) falls
